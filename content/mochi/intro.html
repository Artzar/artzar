Human beings have always been fascinated with shadows. We have endowed them with magic powers, given them personalities, romanticized them, and credited them with the beginning of Art. Pliny tells us that portraiture began when Korinthia, daughter of Debutades the potter, outlined the shadow of her lover as the sun cast it on a wall in order to keep his likeness. Romantic though it may be, we know that shadows of animals painted on prehistoric cave walls were man’s first attempts at graphic art. A shadow of a person, animal, or everyday object will evoke the idea of that person or animal quickly in the viewer’s mind because it is the least amount of information the brain needs to recognize a pattern.


It is only recently that these outlines have become known as "silhouettes," generally referenced as a form of portraiture, but also used as a term for any specific black shape. The word "silhouette" comes from a tightfisted French Minister of Finance, Etienne de Silhouette (1709-69) whose hobby was cutting black paper profiles. The French took their revenge on M. Silhouette for his cheapness by derisively applying his name to anything reduced to its cheapest minimum. Inexpensive trousers without pockets were called "alá silhouette."

M. Silhouette did not invent the pastime. Shadow portraits had been a hobby of the nobility and landed gentry for some years before his birth. Alexandre Dumas described Queen Catherine cutting profiles of her courtiers in La Dame de Monsoreau. Cutting and trading profiles was analogous to people taking snapshots of friends and family with their Kodaks early in the 20th Century. Commonly called "shades" or "profiles," the activity was generally restricted to the upper classes who could afford the necessary paper and scissors and had the time for such activities. Amateur cutters included Josiah Wedgwood and Princess Elizabeth, daughter of George III. Professional instructors often gave lessons in profile art to the children of the nobility. The earliest recorded English cut paper portrait silhouettes are those of monarchs William and Mary in 1699 by Mrs. Elizabeth Pyberg who is believed to have been Dutch.

Swift commented on the practice in Miscellanies:

‘Clarissa draws her scissors from the case

to draw the line of poor Dan Jackson’s face.

One sloping line cut forehead nose and chin,

A nick produced a mouth and made him grin.’

"Shades" changed from a simple pastime of the well-to-do to something more serious by the 1776 publication of Essays on Physiognomy calculated to extend the Knowledge and Love of Mankind. Written by Johann Caspar Lavater (1741-1801), a Swiss clergyman, the book described a simple thesis in great detail: one could read a person’s character by their profile. The book was aimed at those who could afford to buy it: the nobility and landed gentry, the educated upper classes. Lavater gave these people "scientific" proof of what they’d known all along: that they were superior to the lower classes. All worthwhile qualities could be found in the profiles of the upper classes while venality and vice, sloth and drunkenness were found plentiful in the profiles of the lower classes. As may be expected, Lavater’s book was wildly popular. Translated into several languages it went though 150 editions in twenty years. Lavater’s theory was popular not for believers to see what others had in their faces, but to validate what the upper classes knew to be true about themselves.



Lavater wrote, "Shades are the weakest, most vapid, but, at the same time, when the light is at a proper distance, and falls properly on the countenance to take the profile accurately, the truest representation that can be given of man." Since accuracy was vital to the discernment of "true character" a number of mechanical contrivances were developed to ensure accuracy. Emily Jackson, the first Historian of Silhouettes, counts nine different "machines" in one of her books.


Prior to Lavater there were few professional profilists. As Lavater’s ideas grew in popularity professional artists, quick to spot a trend, turned to profile art. Soon every large city and resort had a number of professionals plying their trade. At least 30 working between 1780 and 1820 were skilled miniaturists in monochrome who produced hundreds of striking painted silhouettes on plaster and ivory. Others, seeking a wider audience, charged less and cut paper. A number of artists secured Royal Patronage which also increased the popularity of the silhouette as a portrait genre.

Of course, America had its own homegrown silhouettests, many of whom were contemporary with the Founding Fathers. Perhaps best known are silhouettes from Peales Museum. Charles Wilson Peale was an American Renaissance Man who ran a museum in space he rented in Philadelphia’s Independence Hall. Peale had received a slave, Moses Williams, as payment for a portrait which put Peale in an ethical dilemma. He was a Quaker and did not believe in slavery, yet to free Williams without trade in 1802 would mean calamity for the man.

While Williams assisted Peale in assembling the skeleton of a Mammoth Peale found his solution came when he acquired a "Physiognotrace," which traced and reduced profiles, from John Isaac Hawkins, an English inventor. Williams was taught to use the machine and Peale manumitted him that same year. Williams independently devised a way of folding bank note paper so that four profiles could be created simultaneously, which he sold for 8 cents. He went on to marry Peale’s white cook (who wouldn’t sit at a table with him when he was a slave) and buy a house. Ironically, Williams may have been one of, if not the first free and independent black man to work at Independence Hall.


By 1845, with the ubiquity of "chemical portraiture" or photography, silhouette making went into eclipse for many years, relegated to fairs and other such venues. Regarded as ‘quaint’ and ‘old fashioned’ it remained on the outskirts of portraiture even though a few artists, as recorded by John Woodiwiss in British Silhouettes, did achieve some success with political figures.

The pendulum began a return swing in 1909 when a young American silhouette artist, Le Baron Henri Scotford (1885-1962) was exhibiting at Earl’s Court. A small, stout, bearded man came in and requested a portrait. When showing the client the finished work the client commented, "You’ve made me look like the King." Scotford later learned that he had cut the only silhouette for which King Edward the Seventh ever sat. Thousands wanted to be cut by the young man who had profiled their king and Scotford’s career took off and the portrait silhouette again became fashionable.

While many fine artists were active in the 19th Century it was the 20th Century that produced one of the finest artists to work in silhouette: Ugo Mochi.
