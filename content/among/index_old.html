Bradypus tridactylus, the three-toed sloth, rarely comes down from its tree. With its disproportionately long limbs, a sloth on the ground cannot even support its own body weight, and this predicament reduces the creature to an embarrassing, spread-eagled grope. Safely aloft, however, the sloth maintains its lifestyle of nineteen hours asleep followed by five hours of mild activity, the highlights of which include twig-eating and prolonged gazing. The sloth lives its ten-year lifespan in this way, high in the crooks of trumpet trees. It does not hunt or utter a single sound. It is non-territorial and cannot fight. Eventually, we must ask: what is the sloth for?

It’s no surprise if you can’t picture the face of a sloth. Its closest relative is the armadillo, but what help is that? The sloth has no first cousin and is solitary by nature. It is an evolutionary orphan. But before we find ourselves weeping in sympathy, notice the greenish hue of the sloth’s unusually long, coarse coat. Its fur was made with a certain aptitude for attracting algae, and there it grows, especially during rainy seasons. Along with this primeval colony, the sloth’s coat harbors moths and beetles that live off the algae. The sloth is even known to lick its own fur to get a taste of it, perhaps at those times when pulling a leaf is too much bother. And so, if we take the time, we see the sloth is not alone at all.

Bradypus Tridactylus is endowed with three hollow claws on the ends of each of its limbs. By hooking securely around slim branches, the curved claws allow the sloth to hang comfortably in its natural position: upside down. If you happen to see a sloth hanging this way, you might notice an unusual fact. While the coats of other longhaired animals fall down their sides from a sort of middle part along the spine, the sloth enjoys a part on its belly, with its long hair hanging with gravity toward its back. The sloth is so well adapted to this position in the world that it even gives birth and sleeps while firmly attached by its claws, like a living hammock. Sometimes the creature even remains suspended in its tree for a day or two after it dies.

If you’re trying to make sense of the sloth, look beyond the bias of its name. Could it be this animal is simply more aligned with its dream world? That it climbs high into the canopy because, to its sensibility, the earth is distracting, if not irrelevant? Perhaps the sloth is a deft navigator of its own subconscious and, if it ever decided to speak, would answer our questions in a soft somniloquy.

Because the sloth is a creature mostly overlooked in this world, it is a surprise to find one on display in a handsome domed pergola on the second floor of PT Barnum’s newly opened museum. The animal lay sleeping high up the dead tree trunk propped in its cage, squatting like a child with its long arms folded around its legs and sunlight from the gallery’s high windows warming its back.

“He’s an odd sort of fellow,” commented Emile Guillaudeu, employee of the museum, craning his neck toward the lump of gray-brown fur. “Do you know what he eats?” Just a trace of accent curled the ends of his words. He hooked one thumb in his trouser pocket, and used the other hand to twist the end of his moustache, which in contrast to the white hair on his head was the color of cinnamon. “I hope you can help me, Mr. Pacheco.”

His companion, a South American butcher, nodded. “Perezoso, sí.”

“Do you know what it eats?”

“Hojas. Es que…he eats leaves only.”

“Leaves?” Guillaudeu gave his moustache a tug. “What leaves?”

“Es que…the perezoso don’t live in all kinds of trees. Only one kind. He likes to eat just that leaf.” Mr. Pacheco pointed to the tin bowl of cabbage inside the sloth’s cage. “He won’t eat that. He don’t want to come down.”

“What do you mean, he doesn’t want to come down?” Guillaudeu stamped his boot in an uncharacteristically abrupt manner, waking the sloth. It raised its head in a movement that took almost forever. The two men watched as the animal lifted its arm and soundlessly scratched its armpit, then turned its gaze toward the ceiling.

“How could an animal not ever come down to earth.” Guillaudeu was unaccustomed to rationalizing animal behavior, never having had to account for it during his twenty-five years as a taxidermist. “What should I do?”

“Maybe the perezoso will eat all leaves. Yo no sé. But he needs more branches. He want the branch, me entiende?” Mr. Pacheco raised his arms. “You see? He likes to hang.”

Guillaudeu shook his head. “Who wants a sloth in a museum?”

The two men left the sloth, which had now leaned its head backward over its own shoulders to catch a glimpse of the sun. The morning’s visitors were quickly filling the museum halls with their voices and the sound of their heels on wood. The men passed through the portrait gallery and continued to the main landing. Guillaudeu gave Mr. Pacheco a coin. “Thank you for your help. Gracias.” As they parted, Pacheco made the sign of the cross as he passed the museum’s desiccated mermaid in her viewing cabinet.

Guillaudeu descended the marble stairway against the tide of incoming visitors. At the bottom, near the ticket booth, he passed through a door marked ‘no admittance’ into a narrow hall of offices. He was determined to take up the matter of the sloth with the museum’s new proprietor.

PT Barnum had inherited Guillaudeu along with the museum’s collection of mounted specimens from the original owner, John Scudder. Prior to the takeover, Guillaudeu had spent thirty-six years, his entire professional career, working for Scudder, who had also been his closest friend.

The taxidermist had spoken with Barnum only twice since he had arrived six months earlier. In one of the conversations, Barnum praised the museum’s taxonomic displays and assured Guillaudeu that his services were invaluable to any natural history enterprise. But Barnum then proceeded to reinvent the museum. The original red brick façade turned into a white, five-story advertisement, with a great transparency bearing an image of the mermaid, bare-breasted and smiling, billowing like the sail of a gaff-rigged schooner. Barnum had also opened the balcony that wrapped around the second floor, so that visitors now looked down at Broadway with the same curiosity that they gave the exhibits.

Inside the building, Barnum disrupted the collection by placing random specimens together, like the alligator next to the brown bear. To add interest, he had said during their second conversation. He then eliminated whole groups of animals. Anything outdated must be expunged. Guillaudeu half-expected to be thrown out himself.

He banged on the door to Barnum’s office. No answer. “Where are you?” he whispered, knocking again, and then leaning his head briefly against the doorframe. Guillaudeu made his way back to the crowded entry hall of the museum. His own office was located across from the ticket booth just inside the main doors that opened to Broadway. He paced a few times around the room. It’s not my job to take care of that animal, he decided. Banishing the sloth’s dolorous visage from his mind, he picked up the current issue of the University of Edinburgh’s Scientific Journal.

Guillaudeu’s hero, the French anatomist Baron Georges Cuvier, had just written a discourse titled ‘On the Revolutionary Upheavals on the Surface of the Globe’, which the journal had excerpted. Long periods of inactivity reigned on the surfaces of the earth, said Cuvier, during which whole kingdoms of plants and animals flourished. These epochs, though, ended in cataclysms, usually of fire or flood. Out of the rubble of the old age would arise an entirely new set of creatures, which crawled and flew across the globe until the next apocalypse consumed them. As he read, Guillaudeu had the uncomfortable sense that a natural catastrophe had overcome the museum collection in the form of PT Barnum.
